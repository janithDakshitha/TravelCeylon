Yala

Yala is bordered to the south by the province of the same name and to the north by the province of Uva. Yala is Sri Lanka's second-largest and most popular national park. Yala was one of the first two national parks in Sri Lanka, having been designated as such in 1938 and again in 1990. It's possible to see a lot more in Yala. A jeep safari in Yala national park is a popular tourist attraction. Each of the park's five main sections is called a "block." Only two blocks, dubbed Ruhuna and Kumana national parks, are currently accessible to the general public. As a result of the resident leopard population, Yala attracts a certain type of tourist.

What kind of climate in Yala

Located as it does in an arid part of Sri Lanka, Yala experiences hot and muggy weather year round. The park's median elevation is 125 meters above sea level. Best time to visit Yala

August, October, and December, due to the favorable weather, are the most popular times of the year for visitors. Because the sky is blue and the sun is shining. July is also a month when most tourists avoid Yala. However, July is the perfect time to visit Yala if you're looking for a leisurely and cheaper tour.

Sinharaja

Located in the Sabaragamuwa and Southern provinces in the south-west lowland wet zone of Sri Lanka, the Sinharaja Rain Forest is the largest remaining tract of tropical lowland rainforest in the country and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Surrounded by water on three sides, Sinharaja is a landlocked island. Sinharaja is limited to its northernmost point by the Napola Dola and Koskulana Ganga rivers. The Maha Dola and Gin Ganga rivers flow south and southwest, respectively. Both the Kalukandawa Ela and Kudawa Ganga rivers can be found to the west. An old trail can be found to the east of Sinharaja, close to Beverley Tea Estate and the Denuwa Kanda.
